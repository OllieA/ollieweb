Introduction to Istanbul

Arrive Tuesday, October 30, 1:00p; Depart Saturday, November 3rd, 5:00a

Istanbul is the third largest city in Europe (behind London and Moscow) and is the cultural and economic center of Turkey. It spans the Straits of Bosphorus, this making it the only metropolis in the world that is situated on two continents (see the Wikipedia article for lots more).


Istanbul's districts extend far from the city center along
the full length of the Bosphorus (with the Black Sea at top
and the Sea of Marmara at bottom).

It is an ancient city, known by the Greeks as Byzantium. Constantine made it the capitol of Rome, leading to the name Constantinople. It became the capitol of the Ottoman Empire following the fall of the Byzantine empire to the Ottomans in 1453. Only after establishment of modern Turkey in the 20th Century did it officially change name to Istanbul.

The NY Times maintains a travel guide to Istanbul, amongst other destinations with numerous articles and suggestions for sightseeing, dining and shopping. Here is a beautiful-looking spot they recommended, the Agatha Restaurant, said to be the spot Agatha Christie penned The Orient Express.

Dining

The NY Times has had a number of articles about Istanbul dining recently, including Tasting Istanbul, From Humble to High Cuisine (September 30. 2011)

The Wall Street Journal ran a page entitled Foreign Flavors: Culinary adventures add a little spice to a vacation on 10/8/11. In it was the following passage about Istanbul:

Perched at the crossroads of Europe and Asia, Istanbul boasts a cuisine infused with flavors derived from the far reaches of the Ottoman Empire. Sadly, the guilds of Ottoman-era cooks kept their processes secret, so few recipes survive to this day. That's why you must seek out Tugra at the Ciagan Palace. Here amid sultanesque décor and fabulous Bosporus views, culinary genius Ugur Alparslan recreates recipes found in the Topkapi Palace libraries -- the menu includes dolma (stuffed vegetable shells), lebeniye (sour lentil) soup and wild sea bass kulbasti, followed by an endless parade of sweet little pastries including Turkish delight, said to have been invented in the 19th century to please Sultan Abdul Hamid's harem.

To see how Turkish fare has evolved since Ottoman Rule, embark on a tasting tour of Istanbul's lively backstreets with Istanbul Eats: you'll sample such delights as midye tava (fried stuffed mussels), chicken breast pudding and Kurdish pit-roasted lamb, all the while enjoying unique insights into the history and traditions of Turkish cuisine.

As mentioned above, the website Istanbul Eats ("a serious eater's guide to the city") is filled with tips on exotic dining all over Istanbul, including walking tours .

Sue found TripAdvisor's Istanbul Retaurants page and particularly like #1, #5 and #7. The first one, Metropolis Restaurant is in the old part of the city. Its menu sounds terrific. The second, Imbat Restaurant is also in the old city and is said to have very nice views of the city in addition to an extensive menu. Many traditional dishes covering meats and fishes. Sue's final choice is Albura Kathisma. Again located in the old city, they offer a very extensive seafood and Turkish menu.
